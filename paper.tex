\begin{abstract}
Wireless sensor networks face growing threats from UAV-mounted jammers
that create dynamically moving interference zones. Existing
countermeasures either collapse under relay overload on flat topologies
or require exact jammer location knowledge unavailable to deployed
sensors. We propose a jamming-aware cluster head selection and routing
scheme that estimates per-node jamming risk solely from observed packet
outcomes using an exponentially weighted moving average, requiring no
geometric jammer model. The risk estimate drives a multi-criterion
cluster head election, an adaptive burst-size mechanism with sleep timer
that conserves energy under jamming, a proactive emergency re-election
trigger, and a post-failure stranded-node relay that recovers isolated
nodes during the network degradation phase. Evaluated over 100 random
topologies, the proposed scheme achieves 74.30\% mean packet delivery
ratio, outperforming geometry-aware and threshold-based baselines by
16.05 and 21.76 percentage points respectively, while extending
first-node-death to round 702 and delivering 37\% more total packets
than the threshold-based baseline.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Wireless sensor networks consist of battery-powered nodes deployed
to monitor physical environments in applications ranging from
industrial automation and disaster response to military surveillance.
A critical requirement in these deployments is reliable packet delivery
under adverse channel conditions. Unmanned aerial vehicle mounted
jammers represent an increasingly accessible threat: a single low-cost
UAV can orbit continuously over a sensor field, suppressing packet
delivery within a moving exclusion zone. Unlike a static jammer, a
mobile UAV creates a spatially and temporally correlated interference
pattern. Standard LEACH-style protocols elect cluster heads
probabilistically, make routing decisions without regard to channel
quality, and transmit at a fixed packet-trial count regardless of local jamming
conditions, leading to wasted energy, premature node death, and
extended communication blackouts.

This paper addresses sustained reliable communication in a WSN under
continuous UAV-based jamming. We propose a scheme that acquires jamming
awareness purely from experienced packet outcomes, with no geometric
jammer model, and couples the resulting estimate to both cluster head
election and per-node transmission rate. The key insight is that
exponentially weighted moving average smoothing of observed packet
delivery provides a sufficient, low-noise proxy for local channel
quality under a mobile UAV jammer. When combined with an adaptive
burst-size mechanism that reduces transmissions proportionally to
estimated jamming risk, the scheme conserves energy during jamming
episodes while maintaining high delivery ratios in clean periods.

Fig.~\ref{fig:system_model} illustrates the proposed system model.
A UAV jammer orbits above the deployment field, creating a moving
interference zone. Cluster heads are elected away from the jammed
region using the proposed CHScore metric, and inter-cluster routing
paths are steered away from nodes with high jamming risk.

\subsection{Related Work}

Jamming detection in WSNs has been studied from multiple perspectives.
Kiran et al.~\cite{9915936} apply an exponentially weighted moving
average control chart to per-node signal-to-noise ratio measurements
in a clustered WSN. Nodes whose smoothed SNR falls below a static lower
control limit are flagged as jammed and removed from the network
topology. This approach provides a detection signal but triggers no
structural adaptation: no cluster head re-election, no routing cost
revision, and no transmission rate adjustment, representing the minimum
viable anti-jamming response and motivating the structural mechanisms
proposed in this paper.

Babitha et al.~\cite{11156845} propose a threshold-based countermeasure
(TBC) for flat multi-hop WSNs in which each node monitors its per-round
packet delivery ratio against a static threshold. Jammed nodes are
excluded from routing paths with no clustering and no energy-aware
routing. While the detection logic is sound, the flat topology
concentrates forwarding burden on relay nodes near the base station
under the standard LEACH radio energy model, causing early network
collapse.

Lopez-Vilos et al.~\cite{s23156894} present a self-healing clustering
scheme (FCPA) that uses exact jammer geometry via an
interference-plus-noise (IPN) metric to gate cluster head candidacy and
routes jammed members through cooperative relay paths. FCPA achieves
strong per-round delivery ratios but its IPN gate resets every round
with no memory of prior jamming, and its cooperative relay mechanism
introduces additional energy overhead on relay nodes. In contrast to both baselines, the proposed scheme combines EWMA
temporal smoothing with structural adaptation at both the election and
transmission layers, requiring no jammer geometry information.

\subsection{Paper Contributions}

The contributions of this paper are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A multi-criterion CHScore election mechanism that jointly
    weights residual energy, neighbor connectivity, EWMA-estimated
    jamming risk, and distance to the sink, with a spatial exclusion
    radius enforcing field-wide coverage spread and a proactive emergency
    re-election that restores cluster head coverage within one round of
    any CH failure.
    \item An adaptive burst-size mechanism that reduces each node's
    per-round transmissions proportionally to its EWMA jamming risk,
    with a sleep timer that silences nodes at zero effective trials for
    $N_{sleep}=5$ rounds to cover the jammer arc tail, conserving up to
    100 percent of transmission energy during peak jamming.
    \item A post-failure stranded-node relay that activates after the
    first node death, forwarding isolated nodes' data through the
    highest-energy available member within radio range, with a jamming
    risk filter and per-relay-node load cap to prevent relay burnout.
    \item A comprehensive 100-seed evaluation against two literature
    baselines demonstrating that temporal EWMA learning and adaptive
    energy management together outperform exact jammer geometry
    knowledge, achieving a 16.05 percentage point all-round PDR
    improvement over the geometry-aware baseline and delivering 37\%
    more total packets than the flat-topology baseline, with zero
    communication blackout rounds across all evaluated seeds.
\end{itemize}

\section{Proposed Scheme}
\label{sec:proposed}

This section presents the formulation of our proposed Jamming-Aware
Cluster Head Selection and Routing scheme for Wireless Sensor Networks
(WSNs) under UAV-based interference. The scheme operates in discrete
rounds and integrates jamming risk estimation into both cluster head
(CH) selection and inter-cluster routing decisions.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Network and Jammer Model}

Consider a WSN consisting of $N$ static sensor nodes
$\mathcal{S} = \{s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_N\}$ deployed randomly over a
square field of side length $L_p$ meters, with a fixed base station
(sink) $BS$ located at the field center. Each node has a maximum
transmission range of $r_{tx} = 50$ m; nodes beyond this radius from
every cluster head are stranded and their packet trials counted as lost. The
network operates in discrete rounds indexed by $t = 1, 2, \ldots, T$.

A UAV-based jammer moves above the network along a circular orbit,
with its position at round $t$ denoted as $J(t) = (x_j(t),\, y_j(t))$.
The UAV follows a circular orbit defined as:
\begin{equation}
    J(t) = \left(c_x + r_{orb}\cos(\omega t),\ c_y + r_{orb}\sin(\omega t)\right)
\end{equation}
where $c_x = c_y = L_p/2$ is the field center, $r_{orb} = 35$ m is the
orbit radius, and $\omega = 2\pi/50$ rad/round such that the UAV
completes one full orbit every 50 rounds. The orbit is contained entirely
within the deployment area, and the jammer position is projected to the
ground plane as a simplifying assumption. The jammer degrades the
communication quality of any node $s_i$ within its effective jamming
radius $r_j$. The Euclidean distance between node $s_i$ and the jammer
at round $t$ is:
\begin{equation}
    d_{i,J}(t) = \sqrt{(x_i - x_j(t))^2 + (y_i - y_j(t))^2}
\end{equation}

Each round, every node $s_i$ uses $M$ packet trials to estimate delivery
quality toward its assigned CH. The success probability of each trial
from node $s_i$ is modeled as:
\begin{equation}
    p_i(t) =
    \begin{cases}
        p_{base} & \text{if } d_{i,J}(t) > r_j \\[6pt]
        p_{base} \cdot \exp\!\left(-\kappa \cdot \left(1 - \dfrac{d_{i,J}(t)}{r_j}\right)\right) & \text{if } d_{i,J}(t) \leq r_j
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
\noindent where $p_{base} \in (0,1]$ is the baseline packet success
probability under normal channel conditions, and $\kappa > 0$ is the
jamming decay constant controlling how aggressively interference degrades
nodes near the jammer center. When a node is at the boundary of the
jamming radius ($d_{i,J}(t) = r_j$), the exponent evaluates to zero and
$p_i(t) = p_{base}$, ensuring a smooth transition. When a node is
directly beneath the jammer ($d_{i,J}(t) = 0$), $p_i(t) = p_{base}
\cdot e^{-\kappa}$, which approaches zero for large $\kappa$.

Each of the $M$ packet trials is decided independently; a trial is
successfully received if a uniform random draw $u \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1)$
satisfies $u \leq p_i(t)$; otherwise it is dropped. Nodes observe only
the outcomes of these draws through their packet counters and have no
direct knowledge of the jammer's position or the underlying success
probability.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Jamming Risk Estimation}

Each node independently estimates its local jamming risk using only its
own observed communication outcomes, requiring no knowledge of the
jammer's position or trajectory. At round $t$, the instantaneous Packet
Delivery Ratio (PDR) for node $s_i$ is computed from its $M$
packet trials as:
\begin{equation}
    PDR_i(t) = \frac{P^{recv}_{i}(t)}{M}
\end{equation}
where $P^{recv}_{i}(t) \in \{0, 1, \ldots, M\}$ is the number of
packet trials successfully acknowledged by the CH in round $t$.

To smooth out round-to-round fluctuations while remaining responsive to
UAV movement, the PDR is filtered using an Exponentially Weighted Moving
Average (EWMA):
\begin{equation}
    \widetilde{PDR}_i(t) = \lambda \cdot PDR_i(t) + (1 - \lambda) \cdot \widetilde{PDR}_i(t-1)
\end{equation}
where $\lambda \in (0,1)$ is the smoothing constant. A higher $\lambda$
reacts faster to recent changes; a lower $\lambda$ gives more weight to
historical observations. The Jamming Risk score of node $s_i$ at round
$t$ is then defined as:
\begin{equation}
    JR_i(t) = 1 - \widetilde{PDR}_i(t)
\end{equation}
such that $JR_i(t) \in [0,1]$, where a value near 1 indicates severe
interference and near 0 indicates a clean communication environment.
This score is computed locally by each node every round and requires no
coordination with other nodes.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Adaptive Burst Size Control}

Nodes under active jamming waste transmission energy sending packets that
will not survive the channel. To conserve this energy without distorting
the honest PDR ratio, each node adapts its per-round packet-trial count
proportionally to its EWMA jamming risk:
\begin{equation}
    M_{\text{eff},i}(t) = \text{round}\!\left(M \cdot \bigl(1 - JR_i(t)\bigr)\right)
    \label{eq:meff}
\end{equation}
where $M = 10$ is the nominal packet-trial count. At $JR_i = 0$ the
full $M = 10$ trials are used; at $JR_i = 1$ the count reaches zero.
A node reaching $M_{\text{eff},i} = 0$ enters a sleep state for
$N_{sleep} = 5$ rounds: its EWMA is frozen during sleep and its jamming
risk is reset to zero on wakeup, covering the jammer arc tail and
preventing over-penalisation of nodes after the UAV has passed.
Transmission and reception energy costs scale proportionally by
$M_{\text{eff},i}/M$, and the PDR denominator counts
$M_{\text{eff},i}$ attempted trials so that silenced rounds are not
credited as delivered packets.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Jamming-Aware Cluster Head Selection}

CH election is performed every $K_{elec}$ rounds. At each election, the
number of CHs to elect is determined dynamically as
$K = \text{round}(p_{CH} \times N_{alive}(t))$, where $p_{CH} = 0.05$
is the target CH fraction following the LEACH standard and $N_{alive}(t)$
is the number of alive nodes at round $t$. This ensures the CH count
scales down naturally as nodes die, avoiding unnecessary energy overhead.
Each alive node $s_i$ computes a \textit{CH Score} that reflects its
suitability as a cluster head based on four criteria: residual energy,
local connectivity, jamming risk, and distance to the sink.

\begin{multline}
    CHScore_i(t) = \alpha \cdot \frac{E_i(t)}{E_0}
    + \beta \cdot \frac{|N_i(t)|}{N_{max}(t)} \\
    - \gamma \cdot JR_i(t)
    - \delta \cdot \frac{d(s_i, BS)}{d_{max}}
\end{multline}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $E_i(t)$ is the residual energy of node $s_i$ at round $t$,
    and $E_0$ is the initial energy of all nodes. The ratio
    $E_i(t)/E_0 \in [0,1]$ ensures energy is normalized across nodes.
    \item $|N_i(t)|$ is the number of alive neighbors of $s_i$ within
    communication range $r_c = 15$ m at round $t$, and $N_{max}(t)$ is
    the maximum neighbor count among all alive nodes at round $t$.
    Well-connected nodes are preferred as CHs since they can serve more
    cluster members directly.
    \item $d(s_i, BS)$ is the Euclidean distance from node $s_i$ to the
    sink $BS$, and $d_{max}$ is the diagonal of the deployment area,
    used for normalization.
    \item $\alpha, \beta, \gamma, \delta \geq 0$ are tunable weighting
    coefficients. The coefficient $\gamma$ is weighted most heavily to
    prioritize avoidance of jammed regions, which is the core
    contribution of this scheme.
\end{itemize}

To ensure spatial spread of elected CHs, candidates are selected greedily
in descending order of $CHScore$. Once a node is elected as CH, all nodes
within an exclusion radius $r_{exc} = 25$ m are suppressed from
candidacy. This prevents multiple CHs from forming in the same region,
ensuring coverage across the deployment area. All remaining alive nodes
associate with their nearest CH to form clusters.

In addition to scheduled elections every $K_{elec}$ rounds, an emergency
re-election is triggered proactively when any CH dies mid-cycle, and
reactively when no alive CHs remain at the start of a round. Both cases
reuse the standard CHScore election and pay normal control overhead,
ensuring cluster head coverage is restored within a single round of
any failure.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Jamming-Aware Inter-Cluster Routing}

After cluster formation, each CH must route aggregated data toward the
sink via multi-hop inter-cluster paths. Rather than shortest-path or
minimum-hop routing, we define a \textit{Path Cost} for each candidate
link $(s_i, s_j)$ between two CHs, or between a CH and the sink, as:
\begin{equation}
    C(s_i, s_j, t) = \phi_1 + \phi_2 \cdot \varepsilon_{amp}
    \cdot L \cdot d^2(s_i, s_j) + \phi_3 \cdot JR_j(t)
\end{equation}

where:
\vspace{2pt}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\phi_1$ is a fixed per-hop penalty discouraging unnecessarily
    long multi-hop paths.
    \item $\varepsilon_{amp}$ is the amplifier energy constant from the
    LEACH radio energy model, $L$ is the per-node payload size in bits
    represented per round, and
    $d^2(s_i, s_j)$ is the squared Euclidean distance between nodes.
    The product $\varepsilon_{amp} \cdot L \cdot d^2(s_i,s_j)$ represents
    the actual transmission energy cost of the link, which grows
    quadratically with distance.
    \item $JR_j(t)$ is the jamming risk of the \textit{receiving} node
    $s_j$, penalizing paths that forward data through high-risk CHs. A
    jammed receiving node cannot reliably decode incoming packets
    regardless of the sender's transmission quality.
    \item $\phi_1, \phi_2, \phi_3 \geq 0$ are tunable weighting
    coefficients.
\end{itemize}

The optimal routing path $\mathcal{P}^*$ from a source CH to the sink is
the path minimizing total accumulated cost, computed using Dijkstra's
algorithm on the weighted inter-cluster graph:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{P}^* = \underset{\mathcal{P}}{\arg\min}
    \sum_{(s_i,\, s_j) \in \mathcal{P}} C(s_i, s_j, t)
\end{equation}

% -------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Post-Failure Stranded-Node Relay}

As nodes die, cluster head coverage fragments. Nodes beyond $r_{tx}$
from every surviving CH become \textit{stranded}: their packet trials
count as lost in the PDR denominator but contribute zero delivered
packets. To recover this loss during the degradation phase, a 2-hop
relay is activated after the first node death. For each stranded node
$s_i$ with $JR_i < 0.5$ and $M_{\text{eff},i} > 0$, the relay selects
the highest-residual-energy non-CH member $s_r$ within $r_{tx}$:
\begin{equation}
    s_r^* = \underset{s_r \in \mathcal{R}_i}{\arg\max}\; E_r(t)
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{R}_i$ is the set of alive, non-CH, assigned members
within $r_{tx}$ of $s_i$ not already serving as relay this round.
Packets travel $s_i \to s_r^* \to CH(s_r^*) \to BS$, with energy
charged only for the new $s_i \to s_r^*$ hop (TX at $s_i$, RX at
$s_r^*$); the $s_r^* \to CH$ leg is amortised into $s_r^*$'s existing
cluster communication. Three constraints prevent relay burnout: (1)
the JR filter skips heavily jammed stranded nodes whose two-hop compound
loss would waste energy; (2) each relay node serves at most one stranded
node per round; (3) the relay is inactive before first node death, when
stranded nodes are transient and relay overhead exceeds the delivery gain.

\subsection{Proposed Algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:proposed} summarizes the complete round-by-round
operation of the proposed scheme.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Jamming-Aware CH Selection and Routing}
\label{alg:proposed}
\SetAlgoLined
\KwIn{$\mathcal{S}$, $BS$; all parameters as in Table~\ref{tab:params}}
\KwOut{$\mathrm{PDR}(t)$, residual energy, $|\mathcal{S}_{alive}(t)|$,
$t_{death}$}

\textbf{Init:} $E_i(0)\!\leftarrow\!E_0$,\;
$\widetilde{PDR}_i(0)\!\leftarrow\!1$,\;
$JR_i(0)\!\leftarrow\!0$\quad $\forall\,s_i\in\mathcal{S}$\;
$\mathrm{emergency} \leftarrow \mathrm{false}$\;

\For{$t = 1, 2, \ldots, T$}{

    Compute $d_{i,J}(t)$ and $p_i(t)$ for all alive $s_i$
        \tcp*[r]{Eqs.~(1)--(3)}

    \If{$(t \bmod K_{elec} = 0)$ \normalfont\textbf{or}
        $\mathrm{emergency}$}{

        $K \leftarrow \mathrm{round}\!\left(
            p_{CH} \cdot |\mathcal{S}_{alive}(t)|\right)$\;

        Elect $\mathcal{C}(t)$: top-$K$ nodes by $CHScore_i(t)$,
        suppressing within $r_{exc}$ of each elected CH
            \tcp*[r]{Eq.~(8)}

        Assign $s_i \notin \mathcal{C}(t)$ to nearest
        $s_c \in \mathcal{C}(t)$; flag stranded if $d > r_{tx}$\;

        $\mathrm{emergency} \leftarrow \mathrm{false}$\;
    }

    \For{each member $s_i \notin \mathcal{C}(t)$}{

        $M_{\mathrm{eff},i}(t) \leftarrow
            \max\!\left(M_{\min},\;
            \left\lfloor M\left(1 - JR_i(t)\right)\right\rfloor\right)$
            \tcp*[r]{Eq.~(7)}

        Use $M_{\mathrm{eff},i}(t)$ packet trials toward CH,
        each succeeding w.p.\ $p_i(t)$; count $P^{recv}_i(t)$\;

        $PDR_i(t) \leftarrow P^{recv}_i(t) / M_{\mathrm{eff},i}(t)$\;

        $\widetilde{PDR}_i(t) \leftarrow
            \lambda\,PDR_i(t) +
            (1-\lambda)\,\widetilde{PDR}_i(t{-}1)$
            \tcp*[r]{Eq.~(5)}

        $JR_i(t) \leftarrow 1 - \widetilde{PDR}_i(t)$
            \tcp*[r]{Eq.~(6)}

        Deduct TX/RX energy $\propto M_{\mathrm{eff},i}(t)/M$\;
    }

    \For{each $s_c \in \mathcal{C}(t)$}{

        $\mathcal{P}^*(s_c) \leftarrow
            \underset{\mathcal{P}}{\arg\min}
            \displaystyle\sum_{(s_i,s_j)\in\mathcal{P}}
            C(s_i,s_j,t)$\quad via Dijkstra
            \tcp*[r]{Eq.~(9)}

        Forward data along $\mathcal{P}^*(s_c)$;
        deduct hop-by-hop TX/RX energy\;
    }

    Record $\mathrm{PDR}(t)$, total residual energy,
    $|\mathcal{S}_{alive}(t)|$\;

    \For{each alive $s_i$}{
        \If{$E_i(t) \leq 0$}{
            Remove $s_i$ from $\mathcal{S}$;
            record $t_{death}$ on first death\;
            \lIf{$s_i \in \mathcal{C}(t)$}
                {$\mathrm{emergency} \leftarrow \mathrm{true}$}
        }
    }

    \lIf{$\mathcal{C}(t)\cap\mathcal{S}_{alive}(t)=\emptyset$}
        {$\mathrm{emergency} \leftarrow \mathrm{true}$}
}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Simulation Results}
\label{sec:results}

The proposed scheme is evaluated against two baseline comparison
techniques drawn from the literature, each representing a distinct
level of jamming-awareness in clustered WSNs.

\begin{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Threshold-Based Countermeasure (TBC)}~\cite{11156845}:
    A lightweight threshold-based scheme in which each node monitors its
    instantaneous per-round PDR against a static threshold ($T = 0.5$).
    Nodes whose PDR falls below the threshold are flagged as jammed and
    excluded from routing paths for that round, resuming when their PDR
    recovers. TBC operates on a flat non-hierarchical network with no
    cluster head election and no transmission rate adaptation. In our
    implementation, routing uses energy-weighted Dijkstra (edge cost
    $\propto d \cdot E_0 / E_i(t)$) to distribute relay load away from
    depleted nodes; the original paper's key-management stage is omitted
    as it targets replay attacks rather than jamming. TBC represents the
    strongest detection-plus-rerouting baseline without CH-level jamming
    awareness.

    \item \textbf{Self-Healing Clustering (FCPA)}~\cite{s23156894}:
    A geometry-aware clustering scheme that uses an
    interference-plus-noise (IPN) metric, derived from exact jammer
    position, to gate cluster head candidacy and route jammed members
    through cooperative two-hop relay paths. In our implementation, the
    IPN gate is computed as a binary geometric criterion
    ($d_{i,J}(t) < r_j$), equivalent to the paper's power-based
    threshold under our channel model. The original K-medoids
    re-clustering is replaced by the LEACH epoch mechanism with IPN
    eligibility filtering, preserving the core intent of electing CHs
    from geometrically safe regions while remaining compatible with
    the round-by-round energy budget. The variable-power mode and
    key-management components are omitted as inapplicable to our fixed-
    amplitude energy model. The packet-trial count is fixed at $M = 10$ throughout,
    isolating the effect of the IPN-aware election and cooperative relay
    from any adaptive transmission benefits. FCPA represents the
    strongest comparison baseline: it has access to exact jammer geometry
    every round, whereas the proposed scheme estimates jamming risk
    solely from observed packet outcomes.

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Simulation Setup}

All schemes are implemented in MATLAB and evaluated over $T = 1000$
rounds for each of 100 independent random seeds (seeds 1 through 100),
with each seed producing a distinct random node deployment while the UAV
trajectory is fixed. Reported values are mean and standard deviation
across all 100 seeds. Both baselines (TBC and FCPA) are fully
implemented in MATLAB.

\subsection{Discussion}

\subsubsection{Total Throughput and Lifetime}
The proposed scheme delivers 697,100 total packets across the full
simulation lifetime, compared to 511,200 for TBC (+36\%) and 495,700
for FCPA (+40\%). This metric integrates per-round delivery quality
and network lifetime simultaneously, making it robust to the window
selection effects that affect per-round PDR averages. Fig.~\ref{fig:results}
(bottom) visualises this directly: cumulative packet curves plateau when
each network dies, and the vertical gap at round 1000 reflects the total
throughput advantage. The proposed scheme's curve continues rising until
round~912 (HND), 279 rounds beyond TBC and 85 rounds beyond FCPA.

\subsubsection{PDR Windows and the Lifetime Artefact}
The proposed scheme achieves 74.30\% all-rounds PDR, outperforming TBC
by 21.76 percentage points and FCPA by 16.05 percentage points.
TBC's FND-truncated PDR (82.49\%) exceeds the proposed scheme's
(78.24\%) because TBC dies at round 469, confining its measurement
window to the healthy early phase before significant network
fragmentation. The proposed scheme's window extends to round 702,
capturing 233 additional rounds of increasingly degraded late-network
operation. The round-300 snapshot---when all three networks are fully
alive---shows proposed (82.13\%) and TBC (83.07\%) deliver statistically
equivalent per-round PDR in healthy operation; the all-rounds advantage
of the proposed scheme arises from living 233 rounds longer, not from
superior per-round delivery during the common lifetime. FCPA's PDR at
round 300 (50.53\%) confirms its cooperative relay creates a structural
per-round deficit even when the network is healthy, driven by two-hop
compound loss on jammed members. The proposed scheme maintains zero
communication blackout rounds across all 100 evaluated seeds.

\subsubsection{Temporal Memory Outperforms Exact Geometry}
FCPA holds a structural information advantage: it knows the exact jammer
position every round and uses it to gate cluster head candidacy.
The proposed scheme has no jammer model, estimating channel quality
solely from observed packet outcomes. Despite this asymmetry, the
proposed scheme outperforms FCPA on every metric. EWMA smoothing
provides beneficial memory as the UAV continues along its orbit,
preventing immediate re-election of a CH that just experienced jamming.
FCPA's binary IPN gate resets each round with no memory, creating
the sharp oscillation visible in Fig.~\ref{fig:results} (top, green
curve): each UAV orbit pass switches jammed members between direct
transmission and two-hop cooperative relay, producing periodic PDR dips
that average to 50.53\% at round~300. Second, adaptive burst size
$M_{\text{eff}}$ avoids the cooperative relay overhead that FCPA
imposes on relay nodes, retaining 2.22~J more network energy at
round~300 compared to FCPA.

\subsubsection{Clustering is Essential Under the LEACH Energy Model}
TBC's flat topology validates the clustering premise directly. Without
cluster head aggregation, every alive node routes up to $M$ packet trials
individually toward the sink through multi-hop relay chains. Relay nodes
near the sink accumulate forwarding load proportional to the entire
upstream sub-network and exhaust their budgets by round~469 on average.
This demonstrates that threshold-based jamming detection alone is
insufficient: energy-efficient topology management through clustering
is a prerequisite for long-lived anti-jamming WSN operation.

\subsubsection{Energy and Lifetime}
The proposed scheme retains 34.17~J of total network energy at
round~300, 28\% more than TBC (26.68~J) and 7\% more than FCPA
(31.95~J). All 100 nodes remain alive through approximately round~700
for the proposed scheme, compared to rounds~469 and~572 for TBC and
FCPA respectively. The adaptive burst size is the primary lifetime
driver: jammed nodes that would otherwise drain sending futile packets
instead conserve energy for subsequent unjammed rounds. The post-FND
stranded-node relay contributes a further 1.39 percentage point gain in
all-rounds PDR by recovering isolated nodes during the degradation
phase, with no impact on pre-FND metrics.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented a jamming-aware cluster head selection and routing
scheme for wireless sensor networks under continuous UAV-based
interference. The proposed scheme requires no knowledge of the jammer's
position or trajectory; instead it estimates per-node jamming risk from
locally observed packet delivery outcomes using an exponentially weighted
moving average. Four interconnected mechanisms translate this estimate
into structural adaptation: a multi-criterion CHScore election that
steers candidacy away from jammed regions; an adaptive burst-size
mechanism with sleep timer that reduces per-node transmissions
proportionally to estimated jamming risk; a proactive emergency
re-election that restores cluster head coverage within one round of any
failure; and a post-failure stranded-node relay that recovers isolated
nodes during the network degradation phase. Evaluated over 100 random
topologies against a threshold-based flat-topology baseline and a
geometry-aware cooperative relay baseline, the proposed scheme achieves
74.30\% mean all-rounds packet delivery ratio with zero communication
blackout rounds, first-node-death at round 702, and 697,100 total
delivered packets---37\% more than the flat-topology baseline and 40\%
more than the geometry-aware baseline. The results demonstrate that
EWMA temporal learning and adaptive energy management together outperform
exact jammer geometry knowledge: temporal smoothing prevents premature
re-election of recently jammed nodes, adaptive burst size eliminates the
relay energy overhead imposed by cooperative forwarding, and post-failure
relay recovers stranded nodes without compromising lifetime.

Future work will investigate dynamic cluster head fraction scaling as the
network shrinks in late-stage operation, sensitivity analysis across
multiple jamming intensities and orbit configurations, and extension to
multi-UAV threat scenarios where the interference field is spatially
broader and irregularly shaped.
